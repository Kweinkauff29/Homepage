Top Global Buyers

1. Canada

Our friendly Neighbors of the North. Canada more than any other country sends Florida the most global buyers. It makes a lot of sense when you think about it. Canadian winters are very cold and the Canadian dollar is doing very well compared to the USD. That combined with the quickly rising prices of properties within Canada (probably some of the highest prices in the world) makes Florida a no-brainer for the Canucks. 2020 and 2021 saw a decrease in Canadian buyers due to strict immigration policies both from the United States and Canada but as soon as borders opened, Canadian buyers started flocking here to continue their tradition of foreign investments. Even with the increases in Florida home prices.

In Southwest Florida (Lee & Collier Counties) there were roughly 910 sales to Canadian buyers in 2021. This is almost double or triple the next country. Again, restrictions heavily influence this number but it is still a very incredible figure to put into perspective how influential Canadians are to our local market. For 2021, the total MLS sales were roughly 4,500 for Lee and Collier and although the 900+ Canadian purchases include sales outside the MLS it is still a great comparison to make. Check out the "Global Tips" section to see important information about working with Canadian buyers.

2. United Kingdom

Across the Atlantic Ocean is the United Kingdom - Apart of the British Isles U.K. is the second-largest purchaser of foreign investments in Florida. Over 2020 and 2021 we haven't had a lot of travelers as we typically do because of the difficulty with border restrictions due to COVID but in 2022 we've already seen an uptick in visitors and investments. In 2019 - the U.K. and Europe made for roughly 20% of the total international buyers and we will more than likely see this return for 2022/2023.



Experts are predicting an uptick in U.K.investments going forward mainly due to the departure from the European Union. Regardless of popularity or opinions on the matter, many see Florida as a great way to invest and ride out the uncertainty of the change. Europeans and the U.K. often receive a lot more paid vacation hours than the United States, roughly 6-weeks minimum a year. That means there is a greater chance that a U.K. citizen will use that time to escape the rainy season in U.K. and travel here to visit their second home and enjoy the 200+ days of Florida sunshine.

3. Europe

Europe is a collection of 51 countries with the largest portion of which being a part of the European Union (Flag pictured Above) which comprises 27 countries with a population of 447 million citizens. EU members all share a "system of laws" similar to how the United States works with its Federal and State arrangement. There is free movement and transportation of goods throughout these countries and overall these countries tend to be wealthier to a similar level per capita to the United States. For that reason, that's why, thanks to the European Union, Europe is one of the top 5 Global Investor bases. As mentioned with some of the other countries, the past years (2020 & 2021) have made it difficult for European citizens to move freely to the United States but in the past, this has been one of the largest bases of investors. Experts predict 2022/2023 to see an uptick of investors from Europe, especially after being away for a few years.



The two largest bases of Florida investors out of the European Union are Germans & French. These made up roughly 6% of purchases from Aug. 2019 to July 2020 (View graph in the "Asia/Pacific" Section). European countries have a lot of vacation time and typically have very cold winters and make up a decent amount of Florida's "Snowbirds". Believe it or not, Home prices in Florida are still relatively cheap compared to some prices in big European cities like Paris, Madrid, Lisbon, Munich, etc. Many of us here might be shocked at the rise in price but it's still a discount for our friends across the Atlantic.

4. Asia/Pacific

Asia/Pacific refers to 13 total countries on the eastern hemisphere of the world. Some of the largest investors in Asia consist of China, Japan, and Korea. More than most cultures listed here, these countries are very unique and different in comparison to the cultures in the United States and even from country to country in Asia.



If there is one general characteristic of these countries it's that they see Florida as a great investment for the future. Many of these countries are growing tremendously in recent years and have huge populations. Many of these countries have unique "quirks" - if you know you are working with a client from one of these countries a quick google search or a 10-minute read may go a long way in making sure you are making them feel comfortable. There are a ton of social differences, don't assume how to interact with these clients.

5. Latin America

Latin America consists of 30 different countries in the southwestern hemisphere. Based on the graph below - during the period in 2019-2020 Latin American countries consisted of 5 out of the top 7 Florida residential purchases. Brazil came in second being roughly 7% or roughly one-third in comparison to Canada, our largest local investor.



Brazilians among the other Latin American Countries love Florida for similar reasons to the other countries on the list. They love the unique Floridian weather and the investment opportunities. With these countries - it is a good idea to keep up with the exchange rates of their currency in comparison to the USD as a lot of times the past and future outlooks of those exchanges might play a key role in investors coming from one of these countries. Beyond that, there is unfortunately some instability economically speaking in some of these countries like Venezuela. This causes investors to make decisions with their money and a lot of time Florida makes a lot of financial sense. Like Asia/Pacific countries - read up on some ways their cultural interacts socially. Don't assume they follow the same norms that we do in the United States.

Tips from Around the World

Germany

  • Germans are great hand-shakers, and they like to do so both when arriving and when departing. It is common for a person who is joining a group to shake hands with every single individual.

  • Don’t turn up late for an appointment or when meeting people. Germans are extremely punctual, and even a few minutes’ delays can be offensive. Be five to 10 minutes early for important appointments and be sure to call the people you are meeting if you really cannot make it in time.

  • Germans are extremely environmentally conscious and separate their garbage to facilitate recycling. If your client spots you throwing recyclable glass or paper into the regular garbage, your relationship could be strained for good.

  • Germans take great pride in dressing well, regardless of where they are going or what position they hold. Appearance and presentation is very important to Germans, particularly when it comes to doing business.

China

  • Maintaining eye contact is considered intrusive and a physical embrace is reserved for close relationships. Shaking hands is now commonly used for formal business meetings.

  • Don’t be offended if you offer a gift and it is refused, as it is customary in China to refuse the first offer. Sometimes, the etiquette is to refuse the gift three times, though it may not always take this many tries. In general, the expectation is that a gift is politely refused at first, even if it is desired, and will eventually be accepted after a few offers.

  • In some areas, pointing can be seen as a rude gesture. Instead of using your fingers to point at a person or object, the customary gesture is to use your full hand with your palm facing up and your fingers flat.

  • While it might seem strange to refuse a compliment, it is common to refuse compliments in China since accepting a compliment from the beginning can be seen as a sign of vanity.

Canada

  • Canadians meeting for the first time usually shake hands to introduce themselves and may shake hands before departing, as well. Short hugs are becoming more common for closer friends, particularly women. Kissing remains mostly reserved for family or lovers, though some French-Canadians may partake in the European practice of giving light cheek kisses as part of a friendly greeting.

  • In English-Canada, it’s polite to keep your elbows off the table at all times. However, in French-Canada, men typically rest their forearms on the table during the meal while women rest their wrists. After the meal is done, both parties can put their elbows on the table.

  • Canadians say the word sorry more than any other country in the world. Sometimes, people will say “sorry” to get your attention, without intending to actually apologize. Other times, they’ll say “sorry” before a request, because they understand their request will inconvenience you in some way.

  • The phrase “how are you?” or “how’s it going?” is very popular in Canada. Most of the time, it isn’t actually a question. It’s simply a polite substitute for saying “hey”. Instead of responding with an elaborate answer, you can simply say “fine, thanks” and proceed with the next phase of conversation.

Switzerland

  • When meeting someone for the first time in Switzerland, a handshake will do. You deliver the handshake while maintaining eye contact with that person. Breaking eye contact is considered to be very rude. However, if you’ve met someone more than one time, the greeting is more informal.

  • Because Switzerland is a melting pot, communication styles vary depending on the culture of the speakers. That said, as a whole, Swiss people tend to be polite, reserved, direct, and a little guarded at first. In work environments, social etiquette in Switzerland is to remain formal until explicitly told otherwise.

  • The Swiss value work/life balance in their business culture and generally frown upon contacting an employee or employer outside of working hours. They also take punctuality for business and social meetings very seriously and expect you to do likewise.

  • Social etiquette in Switzerland places a high premium on being tolerant and neutral in all aspects of society.

Brazil

  • In Brazil, Shaking hands has become a more acceptable way of greeting someone, but don't be surprised if you are kissed on the cheek once (in São Paulo) or twice (in Rio and elsewhere), even when greeting someone to whom you have just been introduced. Men do not kiss each other, but greet with an open hug, using one hand to shake hands and the other to grab the man by the shoulder. These greetings are not only used between good friends and family members but are also quite common between business acquaintances.

Mexico

  • Mexican men are friendly, usually men shake hands and if close friends a hug, and if there are women shaking hands is valid but sometimes a kiss on the cheek is given. Mexicans are very polite and helpful, they use the word “please” and “thank you” frequently.

  • Trust plays an important role in establishing relationships, this sometimes could be even more important than professional competence or experience.

  • In Mexico time is not money and it is rude for them to say they are not interested in your product right away. They will praise your product and let you talk even if they do not need it or do not plans to get it. You will have to develop a good sense of orientation here.

  • In Mexico it is normal to finish a sentences saying , no? it is a kind of isn´t it? but be careful, sometimes they say something bad about a subject and it seems that they want you to agree.

Cuba

  • Shaking hands is common for both men and women. Men and women who are friends may kiss on the cheek. Be formal at first and don’t use first names unless invited to do so.

  • The use of slang denotes poor education and is considered vulgar.

  • Cuba has one of the highest literacy rates in the world with almost all of the population (99.8%) being literate.

  • Lunch is the main meal for business meetings and will last around two hours. Dinner is more formal and business is not typically discussed. It is common practice for men to stand when a woman enters the room or leaves the table.
